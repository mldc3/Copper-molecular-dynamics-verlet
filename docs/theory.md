# Theory Background: Molecular Dynamics of FCC Copper

This document explains the physical and numerical background behind this molecular dynamics project. The simulation models a copper-like FCC crystal using a Lennard-Jones pair potential, periodic boundary conditions, the minimum image convention and velocity-Verlet integration.

The goal is not to produce a production-grade copper potential, but to build a complete and transparent computational-physics workflow: construct a crystal, assign velocities consistent with temperature, compute forces, evolve the system and analyse thermodynamic and structural observables.

---

## 1. Molecular dynamics

Molecular dynamics follows the microscopic trajectory of atoms by solving Newton's equations. Each atom has a position $\mathbf{r}_i(t)$ and velocity $\mathbf{v}_i(t)$. If the forces are known, the system can be advanced step by step in time.

The workflow is:

1. initialize atomic positions,
2. assign initial velocities,
3. compute forces from the potential,
4. integrate the equations of motion,
5. measure energy, temperature and structure.

This connects microscopic mechanics with macroscopic observables. Velocities determine kinetic energy and temperature; positions determine potential energy and structural order.

---

## 2. Newton's equations

For atom $i$,

$$
m_i\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}_i}{dt^2}=\mathbf{F}_i.
$$

Using $\mathbf{v}_i=d\mathbf{r}_i/dt$ and $\mathbf{a}_i=d\mathbf{v}_i/dt$,

$$
m_i\mathbf{a}_i=\mathbf{F}_i.
$$

If the force derives from a potential energy $U$,

$$
\mathbf{F}_i=-\nabla_i U.
$$

The minus sign means that atoms accelerate in the direction in which potential energy decreases. If atoms are too close, the repulsive part of the potential pushes them apart; if they are within the attractive region, the force pulls them together.

---

## 3. Why numerical integration is needed

For many interacting atoms, Newton's equations cannot be solved analytically. The trajectory is therefore computed at discrete times separated by a timestep $\Delta t$.

A small $\Delta t$ gives better accuracy but costs more. A large $\Delta t$ is cheaper but may fail to resolve atomic vibrations, producing energy drift or instability. For atomistic simulations, a timestep of order

$$
1\,\mathrm{fs}=10^{-15}\,\mathrm{s}
$$

is typically appropriate.

---

## 4. Integration methods

The Euler method is the simplest scheme:

$$
x_{n+1}=x_n+\Delta t\,f(x_n,t_n).
$$

It is easy to implement but only first order and usually conserves energy poorly in long simulations.

Runge-Kutta methods evaluate the derivative at intermediate points and can be very accurate for general differential equations. However, they are not the standard choice for long conservative molecular dynamics because they are more expensive and not naturally optimized for long-time energy behaviour.

Leapfrog and Verlet-type methods are better adapted to classical dynamics. They are second-order, time-reversible and stable for conservative systems. This is why molecular dynamics commonly uses Verlet or velocity-Verlet.

---

## 5. Velocity-Verlet

Velocity-Verlet updates positions and velocities explicitly. First,

$$
\mathbf{r}_{n+1}
=
\mathbf{r}_n
+
\mathbf{v}_n\Delta t
+
\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_n\Delta t^2.
$$

Then the forces are recomputed at the new positions,

$$
\mathbf{a}_{n+1}=\frac{\mathbf{F}_{n+1}}{m}.
$$

Finally, velocities are updated with the average acceleration,

$$
\mathbf{v}_{n+1}
=
\mathbf{v}_n
+
\frac{\Delta t}{2}
(\mathbf{a}_n+\mathbf{a}_{n+1}).
$$

This method is useful because it gives velocities directly, so kinetic energy and temperature can be monitored. It also conserves total energy well when the timestep is sufficiently small.

---

## 6. FCC crystal initialization

Copper crystallizes in a face-centred cubic structure. A crystal lattice is generated by repeating a basic unit cell. In an FCC conventional cell, the basis positions are

$$
(0,0,0),\quad
\left(\frac12,\frac12,0\right),\quad
\left(\frac12,0,\frac12\right),\quad
\left(0,\frac12,\frac12\right).
$$

Multiplying these fractional positions by the lattice constant $a$ gives the atomic positions inside one cell. Repeating the cell in $x$, $y$ and $z$ gives the finite simulation crystal.

For an $n_x\times n_y\times n_z$ supercell,

$$
N=4n_xn_yn_z.
$$

Starting from FCC is physically meaningful because it matches the equilibrium crystal structure of copper.

---

## 7. Pair potentials

In a pair-potential approximation, the total potential energy is

$$
U_{\mathrm{tot}}
=
\frac12
\sum_{i\neq j}U(r_{ij}),
$$

where

$$
r_{ij}=|\mathbf{r}_j-\mathbf{r}_i|.
$$

The factor $1/2$ avoids double counting, since $(i,j)$ and $(j,i)$ are the same physical interaction.

Pair potentials are simple and transparent, which makes them useful for educational molecular dynamics. However, they are limited for metals because metallic bonding depends on the local electronic environment.

---

## 8. Lennard-Jones potential

The Lennard-Jones potential is

$$
U(r)=4\epsilon
\left[
\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{12}
-
\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^6
\right].
$$

The $r^{-12}$ term models short-range repulsion, and the $r^{-6}$ term models longer-range attraction. The parameter $\epsilon$ sets the well depth, and $\sigma$ is the distance where $U(\sigma)=0$.

The minimum occurs at

$$
r_{\min}=2^{1/6}\sigma,
$$

with

$$
U(r_{\min})=-\epsilon.
$$

For the copper-like model used here, representative parameters are $\epsilon=0.167\,\mathrm{eV}$, $\sigma=2.315\,\mathrm{\AA}$ and $a=3.603\,\mathrm{\AA}$.

---

## 9. Force from Lennard-Jones

For a pair displacement $\mathbf{r}_{ij}$,

$$
\mathbf{F}_{ij}
=
-\frac{dU}{dr}
\frac{\mathbf{r}_{ij}}{r_{ij}}.
$$

For Lennard-Jones,

$$
\frac{dU}{dr}
=
4\epsilon
\left[
-12\frac{\sigma^{12}}{r^{13}}
+
6\frac{\sigma^6}{r^7}
\right].
$$

The force is repulsive at short distances and attractive at larger distances before decaying to zero.

Newton's third law requires

$$
\mathbf{F}_{ij}=-\mathbf{F}_{ji}.
$$

Therefore, the net force in a closed periodic system should be approximately zero up to numerical roundoff.

---

## 10. Cutoff radius

A direct pair calculation scales as $O(N^2)$. Since Lennard-Jones decays quickly, interactions are truncated beyond a cutoff radius $r_c$.

A small cutoff is faster but less accurate. A large cutoff includes more neighbours but costs more. With periodic boundary conditions, the cutoff must satisfy

$$
r_c<\frac12\min(L_x,L_y,L_z).
$$

Otherwise, an atom may interact with more than one periodic image of the same neighbour.

---

## 11. Periodic boundary conditions

A finite crystal has surfaces, and surface atoms have fewer neighbours. To approximate bulk material, periodic boundary conditions replicate the simulation cell infinitely.

If an atom leaves one side of the box, it re-enters through the opposite side. Conceptually,

$$
x\rightarrow x\bmod L_x,
\quad
y\rightarrow y\bmod L_y,
\quad
z\rightarrow z\bmod L_z.
$$

This reduces surface effects and makes the cell behave as a representative piece of an infinite material.

---

## 12. Minimum image convention

With periodic boundaries, each atom has infinitely many images. The minimum image convention uses only the closest image:

$$
\Delta\mathbf{r}
\leftarrow
\Delta\mathbf{r}
-
\mathbf{L}\,\mathrm{round}
\left(
\frac{\Delta\mathbf{r}}{\mathbf{L}}
\right).
$$

This is valid when $r_c$ is smaller than half the shortest box length.

---

## 13. Initial velocities and temperature

The initial positions define the crystal; the velocities define the thermal state. At equilibrium, velocity components follow the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution:

$$
f(v)=
\sqrt{\frac{m}{2\pi k_BT}}
\exp\left(
-\frac{mv^2}{2k_BT}
\right).
$$

In this project, velocities can be generated randomly and then rescaled to match a target temperature. The kinetic energy is

$$
E_{\mathrm{kin}}
=
\sum_i
\frac12 m_i|\mathbf{v}_i|^2.
$$

For $N$ atoms in three dimensions, equipartition gives

$$
E_{\mathrm{kin}}=\frac32Nk_BT.
$$

Therefore,

$$
T=
\frac{2E_{\mathrm{kin}}}{3Nk_B}.
$$

If the current temperature is $T_{\mathrm{current}}$, velocities can be scaled to a target temperature $T_{\mathrm{target}}$ by

$$
\mathbf{v}_i
\leftarrow
\sqrt{
\frac{T_{\mathrm{target}}}{T_{\mathrm{current}}}
}
\mathbf{v}_i.
$$

This gives a controlled thermal initialization.

---

## 14. Removing center-of-mass drift

Random velocities can give the whole crystal a nonzero net velocity. This is not internal thermal motion; it is global translation.

The center-of-mass velocity is

$$
\mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{CM}}
=
\frac{\sum_i m_i\mathbf{v}_i}{\sum_i m_i}.
$$

It is removed through

$$
\mathbf{v}_i
\leftarrow
\mathbf{v}_i-\mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{CM}}.
$$

The usual initialization sequence is:

1. generate random velocities,
2. remove center-of-mass motion,
3. calculate the instantaneous temperature,
4. rescale velocities,
5. verify the final temperature.

This ensures that kinetic energy represents thermal motion rather than drift.

---

## 15. Thermodynamic observables

The simulation monitors kinetic energy, potential energy, total energy and temperature.

$$
E_{\mathrm{tot}}
=
E_{\mathrm{kin}}
+
U_{\mathrm{tot}}.
$$

In a conservative system, total energy should remain approximately constant. Kinetic and potential energies can oscillate because energy is exchanged between atomic motion and lattice deformation.

Temperature is obtained from kinetic energy:

$$
T(t)=
\frac{2E_{\mathrm{kin}}(t)}{3Nk_B}.
$$

For a finite system, temperature fluctuates. These fluctuations are physical and become relatively smaller for larger systems.

---

## 16. NVE dynamics

Velocity-Verlet without a thermostat approximates a microcanonical trajectory, where

$$
N,V,E=\mathrm{constant}.
$$

This is the NVE ensemble. Energy conservation is therefore the central numerical validation. A good simulation should show bounded fluctuations but no systematic drift in total energy.

If the total energy drifts, possible causes include:

- timestep too large,
- incorrect force calculation,
- inconsistent cutoff,
- invalid periodic boundary treatment.

This is why timestep stability is an important part of the project.

---

## 17. NVT dynamics and thermostats

The canonical ensemble fixes

$$
N,V,T=\mathrm{constant}.
$$

A true NVT simulation requires a thermostat. Simple velocity rescaling can impose a target temperature, but if used too aggressively it does not reproduce the correct Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.

A Langevin thermostat adds friction and random force:

$$
m_i\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}_i}{dt^2}
=
\mathbf{F}_i
-
\gamma m_i\mathbf{v}_i
+
\mathbf{G}_i(t).
$$

The friction removes energy, while the random force injects energy from a thermal bath. Nosé-Hoover thermostats instead introduce an additional deterministic degree of freedom that exchanges energy with the system.

This repository focuses on the core MD workflow and uses velocity rescaling mainly for initialization.

---

## 18. Energy minimization

Before finite-temperature dynamics, one can minimize the potential energy. Equilibrium configurations satisfy

$$
\nabla_i U=0,
$$

or equivalently,

$$
\mathbf{F}_i=0.
$$

Gradient descent, conjugate gradient or quasi-Newton methods can be used. In this project, the FCC lattice already provides a reasonable initial configuration, so the main focus is time evolution rather than full structural relaxation.

---

## 19. Radial distribution function

Energy and temperature do not fully describe structure. The radial distribution function $g(r)$ measures the probability of finding another atom at distance $r$ from a reference atom compared with a uniform system.

It is estimated by counting atoms in spherical shells:

$$
g(r)\propto
\frac{
\langle N(r,r+\Delta r)\rangle
}{
4\pi r^2\Delta r\,\rho
}.
$$

The interpretation is:

- gas: $g(r)\rightarrow1$ at large distances,
- liquid: broad short-range peaks,
- crystal: sharp periodic peaks.

For an FCC crystal, low temperature gives narrow peaks at neighbour-shell distances. Increasing temperature broadens the peaks because atoms vibrate around equilibrium positions.

Thus, $g(r)$ is a structural fingerprint complementary to energy and temperature plots.

---

## 20. Temperature effects

At $T=0$, atoms are close to equilibrium lattice positions and the potential-energy distribution is narrow.

At moderate temperature, such as $300\,\mathrm{K}$, atoms vibrate around lattice sites. The crystal remains ordered, but kinetic and potential energy fluctuate.

At higher temperature, vibrations become larger, force and potential-energy distributions broaden, and peaks in $g(r)$ become wider. This indicates increasing thermal disorder.

Because Lennard-Jones is a simplified copper model, high-temperature disorder should be interpreted qualitatively, not as a precise prediction of copper melting.

---

## 21. Finite-size effects

Temperature is intensive, so its average equilibrium value should not scale with the number of atoms. Fluctuations, however, decrease with system size.

Relative fluctuations scale approximately as

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}.
$$

Small systems show noisier temperature and energy curves. Larger systems average over more atoms and therefore produce smoother curves.

Periodic boundary conditions also reduce finite-size surface effects by making all atoms behave more like bulk atoms.

---

## 22. Numerical consistency checks

A reliable simulation should verify:

### Energy conservation

$$
E_{\mathrm{tot}}(t)
=
E_{\mathrm{kin}}(t)+U_{\mathrm{tot}}(t).
$$

The total energy should not drift systematically in an NVE-like run.

### Force symmetry

$$
\mathbf{F}_{ij}=-\mathbf{F}_{ji}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_i\mathbf{F}_i\approx0.
$$

### Cutoff validity

$$
r_c<\frac12\min(L_x,L_y,L_z).
$$

### Timestep stability

A timestep around $1\,\mathrm{fs}$ is physically reasonable. A much larger timestep can inject artificial energy and destabilize the simulation.

### Structural consistency

A low-temperature FCC solid should show sharp radial-distribution peaks. Increasing temperature should broaden them.

---

## 23. Limitations

The main limitation is the use of Lennard-Jones for copper. Lennard-Jones is useful pedagogically, but copper is metallic and metallic bonding is better described by many-body potentials.

A more realistic metallic model is the Embedded Atom Method:

$$
U_{\mathrm{tot}}
=
\sum_i F_i(\rho_i)
+
\frac12
\sum_{i\neq j}
\Psi(r_{ij}),
$$

with

$$
\rho_i=
\sum_{j\neq i}
\rho_j^a(r_{ij}).
$$

This captures the dependence of atomic energy on local electronic environment. Therefore, this repository should be interpreted as a scientific-programming and computational-physics project, not as a quantitatively exact copper materials model.

This project combines the essential ingredients of molecular dynamics:

- FCC crystal construction,
- Lennard-Jones interactions,
- force calculation from a potential,
- periodic boundary conditions,
- minimum image convention,
- velocity initialization,
- center-of-mass correction,
- velocity-Verlet integration,
- thermodynamic diagnostics,
- timestep and size analysis,
- radial distribution analysis.

Together, these elements form a complete educational MD workflow for a copper-like crystalline solid.
