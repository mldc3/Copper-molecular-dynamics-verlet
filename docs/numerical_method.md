## 1. Crystal structures used as initial configurations

Before performing molecular dynamics, we need an initial set of atomic positions. These positions can be generated in different ways depending on the type of material or structure we want to model.

In this project, several structures can be considered: simple cubic (SC), body-centred cubic (BCC), face-centred cubic (FCC), and random structures. The FCC crystal is the main structure used for the molecular dynamics simulation because it is a common structure for metals and it gives a stable ordered starting point.

A crystal structure can be understood as a periodic repetition of a basis over a Bravais lattice. A lattice point is generated from the primitive vectors using $\mathbf{R} = n_1\mathbf{a}_1 + n_2\mathbf{a}_2 + n_3\mathbf{a}_3$, where $n_1,n_2,n_3 \in \mathbb{Z}$.

The volume of a primitive cell is given by $V = |\mathbf{a}_1 \cdot (\mathbf{a}_2 \times \mathbf{a}_3)|$.

In practice, the code uses a conventional cubic cell and repeats it in the three spatial directions. If the number of cells is $(n_x,n_y,n_z)$ and the lattice parameter is $a$, then the simulation box lengths are $L_x=n_xa$, $L_y=n_ya$, and $L_z=n_za$.

## 2. Simple cubic structure, SC

The simple cubic structure is the most direct cubic lattice. The conventional cell is a cube with atoms located at the corners.

The primitive vectors can be written as $\mathbf{a}_1=(a,0,0)$, $\mathbf{a}_2=(0,a,0)$, and $\mathbf{a}_3=(0,0,a)$.

The basis of the conventional cell is simply $(0,0,0)$.

Each corner atom is shared by 8 neighbouring cells, so the number of effective atoms per unit cell is $8 \times \frac{1}{8}=1$.

In code, the positions are generated by looping over the cell indices:

- $x=i_xa$
- $y=i_ya$
- $z=i_za$

with $i_x=0,\ldots,n_x-1$, $i_y=0,\ldots,n_y-1$, and $i_z=0,\ldots,n_z-1$.

This structure is useful because it is easy to implement and understand, but it is not always the most realistic initial condition for metals.


## 3. Body-centred cubic structure, BCC

The body-centred cubic structure has atoms at the cube corners and one atom at the centre of the cube.

A possible set of primitive vectors is $\mathbf{a}_1=\frac{a}{2}(1,1,-1)$, $\mathbf{a}_2=\frac{a}{2}(-1,1,1)$, and $\mathbf{a}_3=\frac{a}{2}(1,-1,1)$.

In the conventional cubic cell, the basis can be written as:

- $(0,0,0)$
- $(\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2})$

The effective number of atoms per conventional BCC cell is $8 \times \frac{1}{8} + 1 = 2$.

In code, for each cell origin $(i_xa,i_ya,i_za)$, the two basis atoms are placed at:

- $(i_xa,i_ya,i_za)$
- $(i_xa+\frac{a}{2},i_ya+\frac{a}{2},i_za+\frac{a}{2})$

BCC is important because many metals have this structure, and comparing BCC with FCC helps to see how the initial geometry affects the energy and the local environment.

## 4. Face-centred cubic structure, FCC

The face-centred cubic structure has atoms at the cube corners and at the centre of each face.

A possible set of primitive vectors is $\mathbf{a}_1=\frac{a}{2}(0,1,1)$, $\mathbf{a}_2=\frac{a}{2}(1,0,1)$, and $\mathbf{a}_3=\frac{a}{2}(1,1,0)$.

In the conventional cubic cell, the basis can be written as:

- $(0,0,0)$
- $(\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2},0)$
- $(\frac{1}{2},0,\frac{1}{2})$
- $(0,\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2})$

The effective number of atoms per conventional FCC cell is $8 \times \frac{1}{8} + 6 \times \frac{1}{2}=4$.

In code, for each cell origin $(i_xa,i_ya,i_za)$, the positions are:

- $(i_xa,i_ya,i_za)$
- $(i_xa+\frac{a}{2},i_ya+\frac{a}{2},i_za)$
- $(i_xa+\frac{a}{2},i_ya,i_za+\frac{a}{2})$
- $(i_xa,i_ya+\frac{a}{2},i_za+\frac{a}{2})$

The FCC structure is the main one used in this project because it gives a compact and stable arrangement of atoms. For copper-like systems, FCC is especially relevant. In the simulation, values such as $\epsilon=0.167\,\text{eV}$, $\sigma=2.315\,\text{\AA}$ and $a=3.603\,\text{\AA}$ may be used for a Lennard-Jones-like copper model.



## 5. Random structure

A random structure can also be generated by placing atoms randomly inside the simulation box.

For example:

- $x \sim U(0,L_x)$
- $y \sim U(0,L_y)$
- $z \sim U(0,L_z)$

This is useful as a comparison, but it is usually not a good initial condition for molecular dynamics with a strong repulsive potential. If two atoms are placed too close together, the Lennard-Jones force becomes extremely large because of the $r^{-12}$ repulsive term. This can make the simulation unstable from the first timestep.

For this reason, crystalline structures are more appropriate when the aim is to simulate a solid. A random structure is more useful for testing the code, checking visualization, or preparing a gas-like configuration at low density.


## 6. General position-generation algorithm

The general algorithm for generating a crystal structure is:

1. Choose a lattice parameter $a$.
2. Choose the number of unit cells $(n_x,n_y,n_z)$.
3. Choose the basis of the structure.
4. Loop over all cells.
5. For each cell, add every basis atom.
6. Store all positions in an array of shape $(N,3)$.
7. Define the box lengths as $L=(n_xa,n_ya,n_za)$.

For SC, the number of atoms is $N=n_xn_yn_z$.

For BCC, the number of atoms is $N=2n_xn_yn_z$.

For FCC, the number of atoms is $N=4n_xn_yn_z$.

This is important because the computational cost of pair interactions depends strongly on $N$.


## 7. Interatomic potential

In molecular dynamics, the interactions between atoms are described using a potential energy function. In this project we use a pair potential, meaning that the total energy is built from interactions between pairs of particles.

The Lennard-Jones potential is written as $U(r_{ij}) = 4\epsilon\left[\left(\frac{\sigma}{r_{ij}}\right)^{12} - \left(\frac{\sigma}{r_{ij}}\right)^6\right]$.

Here, $r_{ij}=|\mathbf{r}_j-\mathbf{r}_i|$ is the distance between atoms $i$ and $j$.

The parameter $\epsilon$ controls the depth of the potential well. Physically, it measures the strength of the attraction.

The parameter $\sigma$ is the distance at which the potential is zero, because $U(\sigma)=0$.

The term $\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{12}$ gives a strong repulsion at short distances. This represents the fact that atoms cannot overlap.

The term $\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^6$ gives an attraction at larger distances. This represents the attractive part of the interaction, such as van der Waals forces.

The minimum of the Lennard-Jones potential occurs at $r=2^{1/6}\sigma$. This is the preferred equilibrium separation for a pair of atoms in this model.

## 8. Total potential energy

The total potential energy is obtained by summing over all pairs of atoms.

Since the pair $(i,j)$ is the same physical interaction as the pair $(j,i)$, we must avoid double counting.

The total potential energy is therefore $U_{\text{total}}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\neq j}U(r_{ij})$.

Equivalently, in code it is often better to sum only over $j>i$, so that each pair is counted once.

For each atom, it is also useful to define an atomic potential energy. In that case, half of the pair energy can be assigned to each atom in the pair. This allows us to visualize which atoms are more or less stable, for example using OVITO.

Atoms in the interior of a finite crystal usually have lower energy because they have more neighbours. Surface atoms have fewer neighbours, so their energy is less negative. With periodic boundary conditions, this surface effect is reduced.

## 9. Pair-distance computation

At each force evaluation, the code computes all pair displacements.

For two atoms with positions $\mathbf{r}_i=(x_i,y_i,z_i)$ and $\mathbf{r}_j=(x_j,y_j,z_j)$, the displacement is $\Delta\mathbf{r}_{ij}=\mathbf{r}_j-\mathbf{r}_i$.

The distance is $r_{ij}=|\Delta\mathbf{r}_{ij}|=\sqrt{\Delta x_{ij}^2+\Delta y_{ij}^2+\Delta z_{ij}^2}$.

This can be implemented using a distance matrix. The diagonal terms correspond to $i=j$, so $r_{ii}=0$. These terms must not be used because an atom cannot interact with itself.

In the code, the diagonal of the distance matrix can be set to infinity. This avoids division by zero and automatically removes self-interaction from the potential and force calculations.

## 10. Self-interaction exclusion

Self-interaction is one of the most important numerical details. If the code tries to evaluate the Lennard-Jones potential at $r=0$, the potential diverges. The same happens for the force.

Therefore, all terms with $i=j$ are excluded.

There are two common ways to do this:

1. Set the diagonal distances to infinity.
2. Use a Boolean mask such as `i != j`.

The first method works well with vectorized NumPy code. The second method is clearer in explicit double loops.

This is not just a programming detail. It is also a physical requirement because no particle should apply a force on itself.


## 11. Force calculation from the Lennard-Jones potential

For molecular dynamics, we need the force on every atom. The force is obtained from the negative gradient of the potential.

For a pair interaction, the force is directed along the line joining the two atoms.

The general relation is $\mathbf{F}_{ij}=-\frac{dU}{dr}\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{ij}$.

Here, $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{ij}=\frac{\Delta\mathbf{r}_{ij}}{r_{ij}}$ is the unit vector from atom $i$ to atom $j$.

For the Lennard-Jones potential, $U(r)=4\epsilon\left[\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{12}-\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^6\right]$.

The derivative is $\frac{dU}{dr}=4\epsilon\left[-12\frac{\sigma^{12}}{r^{13}}+6\frac{\sigma^6}{r^7}\right]$.

Therefore, the force magnitude can be written as $F(r)=-\frac{dU}{dr}=24\epsilon\left[2\frac{\sigma^{12}}{r^{13}}-\frac{\sigma^6}{r^7}\right]$.

The force vector is then $\mathbf{F}_{ij}=F(r_{ij})\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{ij}$.

The total force on atom $i$ is $\mathbf{F}_i=\sum_{j\neq i}\mathbf{F}_{ij}$.

A correct force calculation should satisfy Newton's third law: $\mathbf{F}_{ij}=-\mathbf{F}_{ji}$.

As a check, the total internal force should be approximately zero: $\sum_i\mathbf{F}_i=\mathbf{0}$.



## 12. Cutoff radius

Computing the Lennard-Jones interaction for all pairs has computational cost $O(N^2)$. This becomes expensive when the number of atoms increases.

Since the Lennard-Jones potential decreases quickly with distance, it is common to introduce a cutoff radius $r_c$.

Only pairs satisfying $r_{ij}<r_c$ are included.

This means:

- If $r_{ij}<r_c$, compute energy and force.
- If $r_{ij}\ge r_c$, ignore the interaction.

A typical choice is $r_c=3\sigma$.

If $r_c$ is too small, the simulation loses attractive contributions and the total energy is inaccurate.

If $r_c$ is too large, the simulation is more accurate but slower.

Therefore, the cutoff radius is a compromise between precision and computational cost.


## 13. Periodic boundary conditions

A finite simulation box has surfaces. If we simulate a finite crystal without periodic boundary conditions, atoms at the surface have fewer neighbours than atoms in the interior. This creates surface effects.

To approximate an infinite bulk material, we apply periodic boundary conditions.

The idea is that the simulation box is repeated infinitely in the $x$, $y$ and $z$ directions.

If an atom exits the box on one side, it re-enters from the opposite side.

For example, if $x_i>L_x$, then $x_i \leftarrow x_i-L_x$.

If $x_i<0$, then $x_i \leftarrow x_i+L_x$.

The same is done for $y$ and $z$.

This makes the finite system behave more like an infinite material.



## 14. Minimum image convention

When periodic boundary conditions are used, each atom has infinitely many periodic images. However, each atom should only interact with the nearest image of another atom.

This is called the minimum image convention.

For a displacement component $\Delta x=x_j-x_i$, the corrected displacement is obtained by subtracting the nearest integer multiple of the box length.

In code, this can be written as $\Delta x \leftarrow \Delta x - L_x\text{round}\left(\frac{\Delta x}{L_x}\right)$.

Similarly:

- $\Delta y \leftarrow \Delta y - L_y\text{round}\left(\frac{\Delta y}{L_y}\right)$
- $\Delta z \leftarrow \Delta z - L_z\text{round}\left(\frac{\Delta z}{L_z}\right)$

Then the distance is calculated from the corrected displacement.

This gives the shortest periodic separation between two atoms.

For the minimum image convention to be valid, the cutoff radius must satisfy $r_c < \frac{1}{2}\min(L_x,L_y,L_z)$. If this condition is not satisfied, one atom could interact with more than one image of the same atom, which would give unphysical results.

## 15. Boundary conditions: free surfaces vs periodic system

Two types of simulations can be compared.

With free surfaces, the crystal is finite. Atoms near the boundary have fewer neighbours. This makes the potential energy per atom depend on position. Interior atoms are usually more stable, while surface atoms are less stable.

With periodic boundary conditions, the box is repeated. There are no true surfaces, so atoms have a more uniform environment.

This difference is important when analysing energy per atom. In a finite crystal, surface atoms can be identified because their energy is less negative. In a periodic crystal, the energies are more homogeneous.

This also explains why periodic boundary conditions are normally preferred when the aim is to simulate the bulk behaviour of a material.


## 16. Initial velocities

After building the positions, velocities must be assigned to all atoms.

In equilibrium, the velocity components of atoms should follow the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.

For one velocity component, the distribution is $f(v)=\sqrt{\frac{m}{2\pi k_BT}}\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2k_BT}\right)$.

This means that the average velocity is zero, and the spread of velocities depends on the temperature.

There are two simple ways to initialize velocities:

1. Draw velocities from a Gaussian Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.
2. Draw random velocities from a uniform distribution and rescale them to the desired temperature.

In this project, a simple approach is to generate random velocities and then rescale them.

For example, each component can be initialized randomly in the interval $[-0.5,0.5]$.

Then the velocities are corrected and scaled.

## 17. Kinetic energy and temperature

The kinetic energy of the system is $E_{\text{kin}}=\sum_i\frac{1}{2}m_iv_i^2$.

For a 3D system with $N$ particles, the equipartition theorem gives $E_{\text{kin}}=\frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$.

Therefore, the instantaneous temperature can be calculated as $T=\frac{2E_{\text{kin}}}{3Nk_B}$.

This formula is used to measure the temperature of the simulated system.

If the initial random velocities give a temperature $T_0$ and the desired temperature is $T_{\text{target}}$, then the velocities can be scaled using $s=\sqrt{\frac{T_{\text{target}}}{T_0}}$.

The new velocities are $\mathbf{v}_i \leftarrow s\mathbf{v}_i$.

After scaling, the temperature should be close to the target temperature.

## 18. Centre-of-mass velocity removal

Random velocities can produce a non-zero total momentum. If this is not corrected, the whole crystal moves through the box as a block.

This is not the thermal motion we want to study.

The centre-of-mass velocity is $\mathbf{v}_{CM}=\frac{\sum_i m_i\mathbf{v}_i}{\sum_i m_i}$.

To remove the drift, each velocity is corrected as $\mathbf{v}_i \leftarrow \mathbf{v}_i-\mathbf{v}_{CM}$.

After this correction, the total momentum should satisfy $\sum_i m_i\mathbf{v}_i=\mathbf{0}$.

A typical velocity initialization procedure is:

1. Generate random velocities.
2. Remove the centre-of-mass velocity.
3. Compute the initial temperature.
4. Rescale velocities to the target temperature.
5. Remove the centre-of-mass velocity again if necessary.
6. Recalculate the final temperature.

## 19. Equations of motion

The dynamics of each atom is governed by Newton's second law.

For atom $i$, $m_i\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}_i}{dt^2}=\mathbf{F}_i$.

The acceleration is therefore $\mathbf{a}_i=\frac{\mathbf{F}_i}{m_i}$.

Since the force depends on the positions of all atoms, the equations are coupled. This means that moving one atom changes the forces acting on the other atoms.

The simulation solves these equations numerically using a finite timestep $\Delta t$.

At each timestep:

1. Positions are updated.
2. Forces are recalculated.
3. Velocities are updated.
4. Energies and temperature are stored.

## 20. Euler method

The Euler method is the simplest numerical integration method.

For a first-order equation $\frac{dx}{dt}=f(x,t)$, Euler gives $x_{n+1}=x_n+hf(x_n,t_n)$.

For molecular dynamics, this would correspond to:

- $\mathbf{v}_{n+1}=\mathbf{v}_n+\mathbf{a}_n\Delta t$
- $\mathbf{r}_{n+1}=\mathbf{r}_n+\mathbf{v}_n\Delta t$

The advantage of Euler is that it is very easy to implement.

The problem is that it is only first order. The global error is relatively large, and the method does not conserve energy well in long simulations.

In orbital or molecular dynamics problems, Euler tends to introduce artificial energy drift. This can make trajectories unstable.

For this reason, Euler is useful for learning and comparison, but it is not the best choice for production molecular dynamics.

## 21. Runge-Kutta methods

Runge-Kutta methods improve Euler by evaluating the slope several times inside one timestep.

The second-order Runge-Kutta method can be written as $k_1=hf(x_n,t_n)$, $k_2=hf(x_n+\frac{k_1}{2},t_n+\frac{h}{2})$, and $x_{n+1}=x_n+k_2$.

The fourth-order Runge-Kutta method is $k_1=hf(x_n,t_n)$, $k_2=hf(x_n+\frac{k_1}{2},t_n+\frac{h}{2})$, $k_3=hf(x_n+\frac{k_2}{2},t_n+\frac{h}{2})$, $k_4=hf(x_n+k_3,t_n+h)$, and $x_{n+1}=x_n+\frac{1}{6}(k_1+2k_2+2k_3+k_4)$.

RK4 is very accurate per timestep, but it requires four force evaluations per step.

In molecular dynamics, force calculation is the most expensive part. Therefore, RK4 can become too costly.

Also, standard RK methods are not symplectic. This means that even if they are accurate locally, they are not always ideal for long-time conservation of energy in Hamiltonian systems.

For this reason, Runge-Kutta is useful and accurate, but not usually the preferred method for long molecular dynamics simulations.

## 22. Leapfrog method

The Leapfrog method is a second-order method designed for systems with positions and velocities.

It uses positions and velocities at staggered times.

The velocity is evaluated at half steps, while the position is evaluated at full steps.

The method can be written as $v_{n+\frac{1}{2}}=v_n+\frac{h}{2}a(x_n)$, $x_{n+1}=x_n+hv_{n+\frac{1}{2}}$, and $v_{n+\frac{3}{2}}=v_{n+\frac{1}{2}}+ha(x_{n+1})$.

This method is more stable than Euler for conservative systems.

It is also closely related to Verlet.

The main inconvenience is that positions and velocities are not naturally stored at exactly the same time. This can make temperature and kinetic energy calculations slightly less direct.

## 23. Position Verlet method

The position Verlet method updates positions using the current position, previous position and current acceleration.

The update equation is $x_{n+1}=2x_n-x_{n-1}+a(x_n)h^2$.

This method is second order and has good energy behaviour.

It is useful because it does not require velocities to update positions.

However, in molecular dynamics we usually want velocities explicitly because they are needed to compute kinetic energy and temperature.

Therefore, the velocity-Verlet form is more convenient for this project.


## 24. Velocity-Verlet method

The velocity-Verlet algorithm is the method used in the simulation.

It updates positions and velocities explicitly while keeping good stability.

The equations are $\mathbf{r}_{t+\Delta t}=\mathbf{r}_t+\mathbf{v}_t\Delta t+\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_t\Delta t^2$, $\mathbf{a}_{t+\Delta t}=\frac{\mathbf{F}_{t+\Delta t}}{m}$, and $\mathbf{v}_{t+\Delta t}=\mathbf{v}_t+\frac{\mathbf{a}_t+\mathbf{a}_{t+\Delta t}}{2}\Delta t$.

The algorithm is:

1. Compute initial forces.
2. Compute initial accelerations.
3. Update positions.
4. Apply periodic wrapping if needed.
5. Recompute forces at the new positions.
6. Compute new accelerations.
7. Update velocities using the average acceleration.
8. Store energies, temperature and positions.

This method is chosen because it is explicit, stable, second order, efficient and well suited for conservative systems.

It gives much better energy conservation than Euler, while requiring only one new force evaluation per timestep.

Compared with RK4, it is cheaper and usually more appropriate for long molecular dynamics trajectories.


## 25. Why velocity-Verlet is chosen

Velocity-Verlet is chosen for this project for several reasons.

First, molecular dynamics needs good long-time behaviour. We are not only interested in one short prediction, but in the evolution of a many-particle system over many timesteps.

Second, the total energy should remain approximately constant in an NVE simulation. Euler usually produces strong energy drift. Velocity-Verlet conserves energy much better.

Third, the method gives positions and velocities at the same timestep. This is useful because kinetic energy and temperature depend directly on velocities.

Fourth, the method is computationally efficient. It only needs one new force calculation per timestep, while RK4 needs four.

Therefore, velocity-Verlet is a good compromise between accuracy, stability and computational cost.


## 26. Timestep selection

The timestep $\Delta t$ is a key numerical parameter.

If $\Delta t$ is too small, the simulation is accurate but slow.

If $\Delta t$ is too large, the integration becomes unstable.

In molecular dynamics, $\Delta t$ must be small enough to resolve atomic vibrations. A typical value is $\Delta t \sim 1\,\text{fs}=10^{-15}\,\text{s}$.

The timestep can be tested by running the same simulation with different values of $\Delta t$.

A stable timestep should give:

- bounded energy fluctuations,
- no systematic total energy drift,
- physically reasonable temperature,
- no atoms flying away,
- no sudden numerical explosion.

A timestep that is too large can cause artificial heating, large energy drift and unstable trajectories.


## 27. Units

The simulation must use consistent units.

For Lennard-Jones parameters of copper-like systems, the energy can be expressed in eV and distances in Angstrom.

For temperature, the Boltzmann constant should be $k_B=8.617333262\times10^{-5}\,\text{eV/K}$.

If masses and timesteps are included, the units must be handled carefully so that acceleration, velocity and energy are consistent.

In a simplified project, reduced Lennard-Jones units can also be used:

- $\epsilon=1$
- $\sigma=1$
- $m=1$
- $k_B=1$

Reduced units make the code easier to test, but physical units are better when comparing with real materials.


## 28. Energy diagnostics

At every timestep, the simulation calculates:

- kinetic energy,
- potential energy,
- total energy,
- instantaneous temperature.

The kinetic energy is $E_{\text{kin}}=\sum_i\frac{1}{2}m_iv_i^2$.

The potential energy is $U_{\text{total}}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\neq j}U(r_{ij})$.

The total energy is $E_{\text{tot}}=E_{\text{kin}}+U_{\text{total}}$.

The temperature is $T=\frac{2E_{\text{kin}}}{3Nk_B}$.

In an NVE simulation, $E_{\text{tot}}$ should be approximately conserved.

The kinetic and potential energies may oscillate. This is normal because energy is exchanged between motion and interaction. However, their sum should remain nearly constant.

If the total energy increases systematically, the timestep may be too large or the force calculation may contain an error.

## 29. Ensembles: NVE and NVT

In molecular dynamics, the ensemble defines which macroscopic quantities are fixed.

In the microcanonical ensemble NVE, the number of particles $N$, volume $V$ and total energy $E$ are fixed.

In the canonical ensemble NVT, the number of particles $N$, volume $V$ and temperature $T$ are fixed.

A pure velocity-Verlet simulation without thermostat is usually an NVE simulation.

If we want fixed temperature, we need a thermostat or velocity rescaling.

Velocity rescaling can set the initial temperature, but if it is applied at every step it does not necessarily reproduce the correct Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.

For a more realistic NVT simulation, thermostats such as Langevin or Nose-Hoover can be used.

## 30. Velocity rescaling

Velocity rescaling is the simplest way to control temperature.

If the current temperature is $T_0$ and the desired temperature is $T_{\text{target}}$, the scaling factor is $s=\sqrt{\frac{T_{\text{target}}}{T_0}}$.

Then each velocity is changed as $\mathbf{v}_i \leftarrow s\mathbf{v}_i$.

This is useful during initialization because it prepares the system close to the desired temperature.

However, applying this too often can force the system artificially and remove natural fluctuations.

Therefore, velocity rescaling is simple, but it is not a perfect thermostat.

## 31. Langevin thermostat

The Langevin thermostat models the interaction with a heat bath by adding friction and random noise.

The equation of motion becomes $m\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}_i}{dt^2}=\mathbf{F}_i-\gamma m\dot{\mathbf{r}}_i+\mathbf{G}_i(t)$.

Here, $\mathbf{F}_i$ is the deterministic force from the potential.

The term $-\gamma m\dot{\mathbf{r}}_i$ is a friction force that removes energy.

The term $\mathbf{G}_i(t)$ is a random force that adds energy from the heat bath.

The random force has mean zero, $\langle\mathbf{G}_i(t)\rangle=0$.

The strength of the random force is related to the temperature.

If $\gamma$ is too large, the motion becomes overdamped and unrealistic.

If $\gamma$ is too small, the system does not exchange enough energy with the bath.

Langevin dynamics is useful because it can maintain a target temperature while still allowing thermal fluctuations.

## 32. Nose-Hoover thermostat

The Nose-Hoover thermostat is deterministic. Instead of adding random forces, it introduces an additional degree of freedom that acts as a heat bath.

The idea is that if the kinetic energy is too high, the thermostat removes energy. If the kinetic energy is too low, the thermostat gives energy back.

A simplified extended Hamiltonian can be written as $H_{NH}=\sum_i\frac{\mathbf{p}_i^2}{2m_i}+U(\mathbf{r}_1,\mathbf{r}_2,\ldots,\mathbf{r}_N)+\frac{p^2}{2M}+3Nk_BT\ln(R)$.

The parameter $M$ controls how strongly the thermostat is coupled to the system.

Nose-Hoover can reproduce the canonical ensemble, but it requires careful parameter choice.

For this project, the main integrator is velocity-Verlet, and thermostat ideas are considered as extensions when fixed temperature simulations are needed.

## 33. Energy minimization

Before running molecular dynamics, it can be useful to minimize the energy of the initial structure.

The goal is to find a stable configuration where the forces are small.

The force is related to the gradient of the potential, $\mathbf{F}_i=-\nabla_iU$.

A simple minimization method moves atoms in the direction of the force.

A general gradient-descent step is $\mathbf{r}_{i,n+1}=\mathbf{r}_{i,n}+\alpha\mathbf{F}_{i,n}$.

Here, $\alpha$ is a small step size.

The process is repeated until the force magnitude is below a tolerance.

More advanced methods include conjugate gradient, BFGS or L-BFGS.

Energy minimization is useful because a generated crystal may not be exactly at the minimum of the chosen potential. If dynamics starts from a high-energy structure, the system may undergo strong artificial relaxation.

## 34. Radial distribution function

The radial distribution function $g(r)$ is used to analyse the structure of the system.

It measures the probability of finding another atom at distance $r$ from a reference atom, compared with an ideal random distribution at the same density.

In a crystal, $g(r)$ has sharp peaks. These peaks correspond to neighbour shells.

In a liquid, $g(r)$ has broad peaks at short distance, but the peaks disappear at long distance.

In a gas, $g(r)$ tends to 1 at large distances.

To compute $g(r)$ numerically:

1. Choose radial bins of width $\Delta r$.
2. Compute all pair distances using the minimum image convention.
3. Add each distance to a histogram.
4. Normalize by the shell volume $4\pi r^2\Delta r$.
5. Normalize by the density $\rho=N/V$.
6. Average over particles and possibly over several timesteps.

The function $g(r)$ is very useful because it shows whether the system remains crystalline or becomes disordered.

At higher temperature, the peaks become wider and lower because atoms vibrate more around their equilibrium positions.

## 35. Surface energy and defect energy

When simulations are performed without periodic boundary conditions, surface effects can be studied.

The surface energy can be estimated as $\gamma=\frac{E_2-E_1}{2A}$.

Here, $E_2$ is the energy of a system with two free surfaces, $E_1$ is the energy of a comparable bulk system, and $A$ is the area of each surface.

The factor 2 appears because the system has two surfaces.

The formation energy of a vacancy or defect can be written as $E_f=E(N-1)-\frac{N-1}{N}E(N)$.

Here, $E(N)$ is the energy of the perfect system with $N$ atoms, and $E(N-1)$ is the energy after removing one atom.

This measures the energetic cost of creating a defect.

These quantities show why atomistic simulations are useful: they allow us to connect microscopic configurations with macroscopic material properties.

## 36. Comparison with more realistic potentials

The Lennard-Jones potential is simple and useful, but it is not always the most accurate model for metals.

For metals, the Embedded Atom Method, or EAM, is more realistic because the energy depends on the local electronic density.

The total EAM energy can be written as $U_{\text{total}}=\sum_iF_i(\rho_i)+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\neq j}\Psi(r_{ij})$.

The local density is $\rho_i=\sum_{j\neq i}\rho_j^a(r_{ij})$.

This means that the energy of each atom depends not only on pair distances, but also on the local environment.

EAM is better for metallic bonding, surfaces and defects, but it is more complex to implement.

In this project, Lennard-Jones is chosen because it is simpler and allows us to focus on the numerical molecular dynamics workflow.

## 38. Expected behaviour

For a stable FCC crystal at moderate temperature, atoms should vibrate around their equilibrium positions.

The kinetic and potential energies should oscillate in opposite ways.

The total energy should remain approximately constant in an NVE simulation.

The temperature should fluctuate around an equilibrium value.

The radial distribution function should show sharp peaks if the structure remains solid.

At higher temperature, the peaks of $g(r)$ should become broader and lower.

If the system melts, long-range order disappears and $g(r)$ becomes more liquid-like.

If the timestep is too large, the total energy drifts and the simulation becomes unstable.

## 39. Final summary

The molecular dynamics code follows the classical idea of solving Newton's equations for many interacting atoms.

The atomic positions are initialized using crystal structures such as SC, BCC or FCC. The FCC structure is the main one because it is appropriate for metallic systems and gives a stable initial configuration.

The atoms interact through the Lennard-Jones potential, $U(r_{ij}) = 4\epsilon\left[\left(\frac{\sigma}{r_{ij}}\right)^{12} - \left(\frac{\sigma}{r_{ij}}\right)^6\right]$.

The force is obtained from the derivative of the potential and summed over all neighbours.

Periodic boundary conditions and the minimum image convention are used to approximate a bulk material and avoid surface effects.

Initial velocities are assigned randomly, corrected to remove centre-of-mass motion, and rescaled to match the target temperature using $T=\frac{2E_{\text{kin}}}{3Nk_B}$.

The equations of motion are integrated using velocity-Verlet because it is stable, efficient and conserves energy well for long molecular dynamics simulations.

The main observables are kinetic energy, potential energy, total energy, temperature and radial distribution function.

By changing timestep, temperature, system size and boundary conditions, the simulation can be used to study numerical stability, thermal behaviour and structural order in an atomic system.
